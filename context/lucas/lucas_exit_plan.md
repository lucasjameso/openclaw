# The Exit Plan

## The 6-12 Month Architecture for Corporate Independence, the Strategic Logic Behind Every Decision, and the Emotional Truth That Makes This Non-Negotiable

This document captures the full architecture of Lucas Oliver's corporate exit strategy. Not just the financial model. The emotional driver, the strategic sequencing, the risk management framework, and the precise conditions that must be true before the exit is responsible. This is not a dream document. This is an engineering blueprint with a 6-month target and a 12-month outer boundary.

---

## Why the Exit Is Not About Leaving Something Bad

This distinction is critical because it changes every decision downstream.

Lucas is not fleeing a toxic environment. Facade Access Solutions is a company where he has built meaningful systems, developed talented people, and generated measurable results. He has taken the national sales division from a post-retirement rebuild into a modern, data-driven, automation-enabled organization. He launched the IDS Initiative that became a global framework within the Alimak Group. He leads four regional territories across North America with operational rhythms he designed.

The exit is not about leaving something bad. It is about running toward something that is fully his.

There is a version of Lucas Oliver's professional life that operates entirely on his own terms. Where the platforms, the content, the systems, and the revenue all flow through entities he controls, on timelines he sets, in directions he chooses. Where the calendar reflects his values, not organizational obligations. Where the work he does at 3:30 a.m. belongs to something he will own for the rest of his life, not something that could be restructured, acquired, or redirected by decisions he does not control.

He has spent approximately 30 years building inside other people's structures. He has thrived in those structures. He is grateful for every mentor, every opportunity, every paycheck, every lesson. But gratitude does not eliminate the ache of knowing that your best creative, strategic, and technical work lives inside walls you did not build and cannot control.

That ache is fuel. And the exit plan is how that fuel gets converted into freedom.

## The Timeline: 6 Months Target, 12 Months Outer Boundary

This is not a someday plan. Lucas is building toward independence at a pace that reflects both the urgency of the mission and the discipline of the builder.

He is currently investing 10+ hours per day on Ridgeline Intelligence development outside of his corporate responsibilities. This is not side-project energy. This is founder-level intensity applied to a platform that must reach production readiness and prove product-market fit within the next six months.

The 6-month target (approximately September 2026) represents the ideal exit window: Ridgeline has paying clients, CLARITY has launched and proven commercial viability, and the combined revenue from all three pillars demonstrates a trajectory that justifies the leap.

The 12-month outer boundary (approximately March 2027) exists as the backstop: if product-market fit takes longer than expected, if the book launch generates slower-than-projected revenue, or if the consulting pipeline needs more time to mature, the timeline extends but does not become indefinite. Twelve months is the hard ceiling. Beyond that, the emotional and opportunity cost of deferral exceeds the risk of the transition.

## The Financial Architecture

The current VP of Sales role at Facade Access Solutions provides the income that funds the infrastructure of independence. Every dollar earned in the corporate structure is strategic fuel, not the destination. The salary keeps the household running and the reserves building while the three revenue pillars mature.

**Pillar 1: Ridgeline Intelligence (IAC Solutions)**

Ridgeline is the SaaS revenue pillar. It is a multi-tenant BI platform for specialty trade contractors, built on Vite + React + Tailwind + Supabase + Cloudflare Pages.

**Pricing Architecture (Recommended)**

Based on market research across the construction SaaS and BI software landscape, where construction management platforms (Buildertrend, Procore) charge $499-$1,099+/month, SMB-focused BI tools price at $250-$500+/month for meaningful functionality, and contractor-specific intelligence platforms command premium pricing for operational visibility, the recommended pricing model for Ridgeline is a three-tier structure:

*Foundation Tier: $297/month.* Core BI dashboards, single-user access, standard reporting, and Supabase-backed real-time data. This tier targets the owner-operator who needs visibility into their numbers but is not yet ready for a full platform investment. It serves as the entry point and the conversion mechanism.

*Professional Tier: $497/month.* Full BI suite, team access (up to 5 users), custom report builder, AI-powered insights, and priority support. This is the hero tier, the one most clients should land on, positioned as the clear value sweet spot between basic and premium.

*Enterprise Tier: $997/month.* Multi-location support, unlimited users, API access, white-label options, dedicated onboarding, and advanced AI analytics. This tier targets larger specialty contractors with multiple crews, offices, or divisions.

The pricing logic: at the Professional tier ($497/month average), 10 clients generates approximately $5K/month, 20 clients generates approximately $10K/month, and 30 clients approaches $15K/month. These are realistic client counts for a 6-12 month window, and each increment materially advances the exit math.

Annual billing should offer a 15-20% discount (effectively 10-12 months for the price of 12), which improves cash flow predictability and reduces churn risk. The annual option also locks in early adopters and demonstrates revenue durability to Lucas's own exit math.

The current state: first POC client (Clint in Wake Forest, NC) in active development. Lucas is building at 10+ hours per day outside corporate hours. The product must prove its value proposition with Clint, convert to a paying subscription, and then replicate with additional clients before the exit math closes.

## What Must Be True Before the Exit Is Responsible

Lucas will not exit before the systems are proven. Structure over wishful thinking. This is the non-negotiable filter that separates a responsible exit from a reckless leap.

**Revenue validation.** The three pillars must demonstrate, through actual revenue data, not projections, that the combined income meets the minimum threshold. This threshold includes: equivalent take-home income to the corporate role, health insurance coverage for the family, retirement contribution capacity, and a minimum 3-month cash reserve for revenue volatility. The reserve requirement is compressed from a more conservative target because the exit timeline is compressed, and waiting for a larger reserve would push beyond the 12-month outer boundary.

**Product-market fit for Ridgeline.** The POC must convert. At least two to three additional clients must be paying or committed. The product must solve a real problem that trade contractors are willing to pay for on a recurring basis. If Ridgeline does not prove product-market fit within the 6-month target window, the timeline extends toward the 12-month boundary, but it does not extend beyond it.

**Brand traction for Build What Lasts.** The CLARITY launch must demonstrate commercial viability across all three formats. The LinkedIn audience must reach the 10,000 target, proving distribution capacity. The marketing infrastructure across book platforms, content platforms, and advertising channels must be operational and producing measurable results.

**Operational independence.** The systems must run without Lucas being the only person who can operate them. If Ridgeline requires him to be the sole support engineer, the product is not ready. The exit is about freedom, and freedom requires systems that function without constant personal intervention.

## The Emotional Cost of Deferring the Exit

Every month inside the corporate structure is a month where the systems Lucas is building for his own future compete for time and energy with obligations he did not design. The emotional cost of this is real and cumulative.

It is the reality of building Ridgeline at 10+ hours per day and then showing up for a full corporate schedule on the other side of the clock. It is the knowledge that the best hours of his creative energy are split between two worlds. It is the quiet frustration of knowing that the LinkedIn post, the book chapter, the next Ridgeline feature, all the work that builds toward independence, fits into the margins of a schedule designed around corporate deliverables.

Lucas does not allow this frustration to become bitterness. Bitterness is corrosive and he will not let it touch his work, his relationships, or his leadership. But he refuses to pretend the weight does not exist. Every month deferred is a month of opportunity cost. The 6-month target exists because Lucas has the ability to get there sooner than most people would think possible, and the intensity of his current build pace is the evidence.

## The First 90 Days of Full Independence

When the exit occurs, the first 90 days are not a vacation. They are the most critical operating period of the entire plan.

**Days 1-30: Operational stabilization.** Confirm all revenue pipelines are active. Establish the daily operating rhythm without corporate structure providing the backbone. Set up the cadence for Ridgeline client management, Build What Lasts content production, and IAC consulting delivery. This is the phase where the absence of someone else's structure can feel disorienting, and the systems must fill that gap.

**Days 31-60: Growth acceleration.** With 100% of creative and strategic energy directed at the three pillars, this is the window where compounding begins. Double down on Ridgeline client acquisition. Launch the next product in the Build What Lasts ecosystem. Increase LinkedIn content cadence. Convert the energy that was previously split between corporate and independent work into focused execution.

**Days 61-90: Evaluation and adjustment.** Honest assessment of the financial model against actual results. Where is revenue tracking? Where are the gaps? What needs to change? This is the audit that determines whether the exit trajectory is healthy or whether course corrections are needed.
